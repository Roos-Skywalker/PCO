GPUs now forced to respect the laws of reality

Disclaimer: This is an article for fun, not a scientific dissertation.

Do you remember that romantic evening with your date on the safe beach of Flushing? That's the one; that's when you tie the knot. Literally.

So the two of you take the edges of your shirts and tie them together —aww how romantic!— until the fabric starts convulsing, distorting and clipping through each other in a surrealistic nightmare.

You wake up. Immersion broken. Your disappointment immeasurable. Your day ruined. Penetration-Free guaranteed.


Six scientists invented a new mathematic method called Offset Geometric Contact or OGC for short, that promises to fix the penetration of digital objects during simulations.

As you may know; computers render 3D graphics as triangular polygons that form a 3D shape.

These triangles have three types of faces: Triangle, Edge and Vertex.

Imagine the triangle face to be the flat, triangular surfaces of the 3D triangle shape. The edge face where those surfaces meet. Whereas the vertex face can be imagined as the tips of multiple surfaces meeting.

The OGC method creates invisible opposing shapes on each face type that function similarly to hitboxes in video games.


But these 'hitboxes' can only push directly outwards—unlike the currently used method IPC, whose 'hitboxes' offset in all directions.

IPC, aka Incremental Potential Contact, remedies pushing itself apart by greatly reducing the size of the 'hitbox'. (Or when using its proper name: the Contact Model)

In low-velocity simulations OGC was observed to be up to 300 times faster than IPC. OGC achieves these speeds through its Contact Model and simplified mathematics.

Because its 'hitboxes' or its Contact Model is bigger, the GPU needs fewer collision checks.

Thanks to the Contact Model only pushing outwards, 3D meshes can correct themselves to fit the shape they were intended to be after manipulation or incorrect initialisation.


OGC still has issues with high-velocity collisions causing minor penetration and performance decreases, but the future looks bright for realistic cape physics.
